LAND ACCESS & PERMISSIONS GUIDE

INTRODUCTION

Securing land access and proper permissions is a critical first step in any trail building project. This guide provides mountain bike organizations, trail advocates, and community groups with a comprehensive framework for identifying land ownership, approaching landowners, negotiating access agreements, and maintaining positive relationships throughout the trail development process.

UNDERSTANDING LAND OWNERSHIP TYPES

Public Land

Federal Land

National Forests (USFS)

  • Managed under multiple-use mandate
  • Requires formal proposal process
  • Subject to NEPA environmental review
  • Often allows volunteer agreements for maintenance

Bureau of Land Management (BLM)

  • Similar to USFS but often with more flexible use policies
  • Recreation Area Management Plans guide development
  • Resource Management Plans define allowed uses

National Park Service (NPS)

  • Most restrictive federal land management
  • Mountain biking access varies by park unit
  • Special use permits required for events/activities
State Land

State Parks

  • Policies vary significantly by state
  • Often have established trail development processes
  • May require partnership agreements

State Forests

  • Generally more open to recreation development
  • Often require formal proposals
  • May have timber management priorities

Wildlife Management Areas

  • Primary focus on habitat conservation
  • May have seasonal restrictions
  • Requires coordination with wildlife biologists
Local Government Land

County Parks

  • Often most receptive to local trail initiatives
  • May have recreation master plans guiding development
  • Usually require formal proposal to parks department

Municipal Land

  • City parks departments typically manage
  • Urban trail connections often prioritized
  • May have specific liability concerns

Private Land

Individual Landowners

Residential Property Owners

  • Personal relationships critical
  • Concerns often focus on privacy and liability
  • May be motivated by community benefit

Agricultural Landowners

  • Concerns about crop/livestock impacts
  • Seasonal access considerations
  • May have tax incentive opportunities

Timber/Resource Landowners

  • Often open to recreation if compatible with operations
  • May have rotating harvest schedules
  • Typically require formal agreements
Institutional Landowners

Land Trusts/Conservation Organizations

  • Mission-driven organizations
  • Conservation easements may restrict uses
  • Often supportive if trails align with conservation goals

Corporate Landowners

  • May have formal proposal requirements
  • Often concerned with liability and public relations
  • May seek community benefit recognition

Utility Corridors

  • Power lines, pipelines, water districts
  • Often have specific use restrictions
  • May require engineering review

Research Phase

1

Identify Ownership

  • County tax assessor records
  • GIS parcel maps
  • Land ownership databases
  • Title searches for complex situations
2

Understand Regulations

  • Zoning restrictions
  • Easements and right-of-ways
  • Environmental designations
  • Historical/cultural site restrictions
3

Identify Decision Makers

  • Property managers for corporate land
  • Direct owners for private property
  • Board/committee contacts for institutions

  • Maintenance commitments

NEGOTIATING ACCESS AGREEMENTS

Types of Agreements

Low Security

Handshake Agreements

  • Informal and based on trust
  • Not legally binding
  • Vulnerable to ownership changes
  • Not recommended for significant investments
Low Security

License Agreements

  • Temporary permission to use property
  • Typically revocable with notice
  • Limited legal protection
  • Good for trial periods
High Security

Easements

  • Permanent right to use portion of property
  • Recorded with property deed
  • Survives property sale
  • May have tax benefits for landowner
Medium Security

Leases

  • Defined term (typically 5-20 years)
  • May include payment or in-kind services
  • Specific conditions and renewal terms
  • Good balance of security and flexibility
High Security

Land Purchase

  • Full ownership and control
  • Highest initial cost
  • Long-term maintenance responsibility
  • Maximum security for trail investment

Key Agreement Components

Access Terms
  • Specific areas included/excluded
  • Allowed uses and restrictions
  • Hours/seasons of operation
  • Special event provisions
Duration and Renewal
  • Agreement term length
  • Renewal process and conditions
  • Termination clauses and notice periods
  • Dispute resolution process
Liability and Insurance
  • Indemnification language
  • Insurance requirements
  • Risk management protocols
  • Signage requirements
Maintenance Responsibilities
  • Regular maintenance duties
  • Emergency repair protocols
  • Improvement approval process
  • Inspection and reporting requirements
Financial Considerations
  • Fee structure (if applicable)
  • In-kind services
  • Tax implications
  • Cost-sharing arrangements

LEGAL CONSIDERATIONS

Liability Protection

Recreational Use Statutes

  • State-specific protections for landowners
  • Typically requires no fee for access
  • Protects against ordinary negligence
  • Does not protect against gross negligence

Insurance Coverage

  • General liability policies
  • Umbrella policies for larger coverage
  • Named insured provisions
  • Event-specific coverage

Risk Management

  • Trail difficulty ratings
  • Warning signage
  • Technical feature alternatives
  • Regular safety inspections

Environmental Compliance

CONCLUSION

Securing land access and permissions requires patience, professionalism, and persistence. By understanding landowner perspectives, creating mutually beneficial agreements, and maintaining positive relationships, trail organizations can build sustainable access that benefits communities for generations to come. Remember that each land access situation is unique and may require customized approaches based on specific landowner needs and local conditions.
