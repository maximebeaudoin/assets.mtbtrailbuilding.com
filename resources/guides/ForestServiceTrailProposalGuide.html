FOREST SERVICE TRAIL PROPOSAL GUIDE

INTRODUCTION

This guide provides a comprehensive framework for developing and submitting trail proposals to the United States Forest Service (USFS). It is designed to help mountain bike organizations, trail advocates, and community groups navigate the USFS approval process effectively.

UNDERSTANDING THE FOREST SERVICE

Agency Structure

The USFS is organized into several levels:

  • National Headquarters: Sets overall policy and direction
  • Regional Offices: Oversee multiple National Forests
  • Forest Supervisor's Office: Manages individual National Forests
  • Ranger Districts: Local offices that manage specific areas within a National Forest

For trail proposals, your primary contacts will typically be at the Ranger District level, though larger projects may involve the Forest Supervisor's Office.

Key Personnel

Identify and build relationships with:

  • District Ranger: The decision-maker for most trail proposals
  • Recreation Staff Officer: Oversees recreation programs and planning
  • Trails Specialist/Coordinator: Technical expert on trail design and management
  • NEPA Coordinator: Manages environmental review processes
  • Public Affairs Officer: Handles public communications and engagement

FOREST SERVICE PLANNING FRAMEWORK

Land Management Plans

All activities on National Forest land must be consistent with the applicable Land and Resource Management Plan (Forest Plan). Before developing a proposal, review the current Forest Plan to ensure your project aligns with management direction.

Travel Management

The USFS manages motorized and non-motorized travel through the Travel Management Rule. Your proposal must comply with existing Travel Management Decisions or may need to be incorporated into Travel Management planning.

NEPA Process

The National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) requires federal agencies to assess environmental impacts before making decisions. Trail proposals typically require NEPA analysis, which may take the form of:

  • Categorical Exclusion (CE): For projects with minimal environmental impact
  • Environmental Assessment (EA): For projects with uncertain impacts
  • Environmental Impact Statement (EIS): For major projects with significant impacts

DEVELOPING YOUR PROPOSAL

Pre-Proposal Research

  1. Review Existing Plans

    • Forest Plan
    • Travel Management Plan
    • Recreation Opportunity Spectrum (ROS) classifications
    • Special area designations (Wilderness, Research Natural Areas, etc.)

  2. Identify Management Constraints

    • Protected species and habitats
    • Cultural/historical resources
    • Riparian areas and wetlands
    • Existing uses and user groups

  3. Understand Local Priorities

    • Meet with USFS staff to discuss recreation goals
    • Review Capital Improvement Plans
    • Identify maintenance backlogs and capacity issues

Proposal Components

A complete trail proposal should include:

1. Project Overview

  • Project name and location
  • Proposing organization(s)
  • Primary contact information
  • Brief summary of proposed action

2. Purpose and Need

  • Clearly articulate why the project is needed
  • Identify benefits to the public and the Forest Service
  • Explain how the project supports Forest Plan goals

3. Detailed Project Description

  • Trail specifications (length, width, difficulty, user types)
  • Construction methods and standards
  • Proposed timeline and phasing
  • Maps and GIS data

4. Resource Considerations

  • Potential environmental impacts and mitigation measures
  • Cultural resource considerations
  • Wildlife and plant considerations
  • Soil and water quality protections

5. Stakeholder Engagement

  • Public outreach conducted or planned
  • Support from local communities
  • Coordination with other user groups
  • Letters of support

6. Implementation Plan

  • Construction approach (contractor, volunteer, USFS, or combination)
  • Funding sources and budget
  • Long-term maintenance strategy
  • Monitoring plan

SUBMISSION AND REVIEW PROCESS

Initial Contact

  1. Schedule an informal meeting with district staff to discuss your concept
  2. Incorporate their feedback before formal submission
  3. Identify the appropriate submission process (some forests have specific forms or procedures)

Formal Submission

Submit your proposal according to local requirements, typically including:

  • Cover letter
  • Complete proposal package
  • Supporting documentation
  • GIS data in compatible format

Review Timeline

The review process typically includes:

  1. Initial Screening (1-2 months): Determine if proposal merits further consideration
  2. Preliminary Assessment (2-3 months): Evaluate feasibility and resource concerns
  3. NEPA Process (6-24+ months): Environmental analysis and public comment
  4. Decision (1-2 months): Final approval or denial
  5. Implementation Planning (2-3 months): Develop detailed work plans

KEYS TO SUCCESS

Building Agency Partnerships

  • Start Early: Begin discussions with USFS staff years before desired implementation
  • Be Patient: Understand that agency processes take time
  • Be Helpful: Look for ways to support USFS goals and capacity
  • Be Consistent: Maintain regular communication and follow through on commitments

Addressing Common Concerns

  • Maintenance Capacity: Develop robust volunteer programs and maintenance endowments
  • User Conflicts: Design trails to minimize conflicts and educate users
  • Environmental Impacts: Propose sustainable designs that minimize resource damage
  • Cumulative Effects: Consider how your proposal fits with existing trail systems

Leveraging Resources

  • Grants: Identify funding sources that can support both planning and implementation
  • Partnerships: Collaborate with other organizations to strengthen proposals
  • Professional Support: Consider hiring consultants for complex aspects (NEPA, design)
  • Agency Programs: Explore USFS programs like Challenge Cost Share Agreements

CASE STUDIES

Success Story: Collaboration Creek Trail System

A local mountain bike club successfully partnered with the Forest Service to develop a 25-mile trail system by:

  • Forming a collaborative working group with diverse stakeholders
  • Funding environmental studies to reduce agency workload
  • Providing certified volunteer trail crews for construction
  • Establishing a maintenance endowment fund

Lessons Learned: Ridgeline Project Challenges

A proposed trail project faced significant delays due to:
  • Insufficient early coordination with wildlife biologists
  • Inadequate stakeholder engagement leading to public opposition
  • Unrealistic timeline expectations
  • Lack of clear maintenance commitments
