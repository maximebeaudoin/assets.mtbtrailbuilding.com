WATERSHED ANALYSIS GUIDE FOR TRAIL PLANNING

INTRODUCTION

Water is both a critical resource to protect and one of the most significant factors affecting trail sustainability. This guide provides trail planners and builders with methods to analyze watershed characteristics, identify drainage patterns, and design trails that minimize impacts on water resources while ensuring long-term trail sustainability.

UNDERSTANDING WATERSHEDS

What is a Watershed?

A watershed (also called a catchment or drainage basin) is an area of land where all precipitation that falls within it drains to a common outlet such as a stream, river, lake, or wetland. Watersheds exist at multiple scales, from small local drainages to massive river systems.

Watershed Components

  • Ridgelines: Define watershed boundaries
  • Slopes: Determine water flow direction and velocity
  • Valleys: Collect and channel water
  • Streams and Rivers: Transport water through the landscape
  • Wetlands: Store and filter water
  • Groundwater: Subsurface water movement
  • Soil and Vegetation: Influence infiltration and runoff

Why Watershed Analysis Matters for Trails

  • Trail Sustainability: Proper drainage is essential for trail longevity
  • Environmental Protection: Minimizing erosion and sedimentation protects water quality
  • Regulatory Compliance: Many water features have legal protections
  • User Experience: Dry trails provide better recreation experiences
  • Maintenance Efficiency: Well-drained trails require less maintenance

WATERSHED MAPPING TECHNIQUES

Desktop Analysis

Topographic Map Interpretation

  1. Identify ridgelines (watershed boundaries)
  2. Locate streams, rivers, and water bodies
  3. Note contour line patterns indicating:
    • Valleys and drainages
    • Slope steepness
    • Aspect (direction slopes face)
  4. Mark potential problem areas:
    • Stream crossings
    • Steep slopes
    • Confined valleys

Digital Mapping Tools

  • GIS Software (QGIS, ArcGIS)
    • Watershed delineation tools
    • Flow accumulation analysis
    • Slope and aspect mapping
    • Hydrologic modeling
  • Online Resources
    • USGS StreamStats (streamstats.usgs.gov)
    • EPA How's My Waterway (mywaterway.epa.gov)
    • USGS National Hydrography Dataset
    • USGS 3DEP Elevation Program data

LiDAR Analysis

LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) data provides high-resolution elevation information that can reveal:

  • Subtle drainage patterns not visible on standard maps
  • Small streams and seeps
  • Microtopography affecting water flow
  • Previous erosion patterns

Field Assessment

Visual Watershed Assessment

  1. Walk the proposed trail corridor in different weather conditions
  2. Identify and map:
    • Springs and seeps
    • Ephemeral (seasonal) streams
    • Wetlands and saturated soils
    • Existing drainage patterns
    • Evidence of past flooding or high water

Soil and Vegetation Indicators

  • Hydric Soils: Dark colors, mottling, or gleying indicate periodic saturation
  • Hydrophytic Vegetation: Certain plants indicate wet conditions:
    • Sedges, rushes, cattails
    • Willows, alders, and other water-loving trees
    • Mosses and certain ferns
  • Erosion Signs: Rills, gullies, or exposed roots indicate active erosion

Stream Crossing Assessment

For each potential stream crossing, document:

  1. Channel width, depth, and substrate
  2. Bank stability and height
  3. Evidence of high water levels
  4. Upstream and downstream conditions
  5. Aquatic habitat characteristics
  6. Potential crossing structure options

ANALYZING WATERSHED CHARACTERISTICS

Drainage Density

Drainage density is the total length of streams divided by watershed area. Higher density indicates:

  • More frequent stream crossings needed
  • Greater potential for water-related trail issues
  • More complex drainage management required

Slope Analysis

Categorize slopes in the project area:

  • Gentle (0-10%): Generally good for trails, may have drainage challenges
  • Moderate (10-30%): Ideal for sustainable trails with proper design
  • Steep (30-50%): Challenging but possible with specialized techniques
  • Very Steep (>50%): Generally avoid or use specialized structures

Aspect and Sun Exposure

Aspect (the direction a slope faces) affects:

  • Snow melt timing: North-facing slopes retain snow longer
  • Soil moisture: North-facing slopes typically remain wetter
  • Vegetation: Different plant communities affect drainage
  • Trail drying: South-facing slopes dry faster after rain

Soil Infiltration Capacity

Assess how quickly water enters the soil:

  • High infiltration (sandy soils): Less surface runoff but more trail displacement
  • Moderate infiltration (loamy soils): Good balance for trails
  • Low infiltration (clay soils): More surface runoff, greater erosion potential

Flood Frequency and Magnitude

Understand the flooding regime:

  1. Research historical flood records
  2. Look for high water marks on trees or banks
  3. Check for debris accumulation indicating flood levels
  4. Consider seasonal patterns (snowmelt, monsoon seasons, etc.)
  5. Account for climate change projections

IDENTIFYING SENSITIVE WATER RESOURCES

Regulatory Considerations

Many water resources have legal protections:

  • Wetlands: Often protected under Clean Water Act Section 404
  • Streams: May require permits for crossings or nearby disturbance
  • Riparian Areas: Special management zones in many jurisdictions
  • Water Supply Areas: Additional protections for drinking water sources

Ecological Value Assessment

Evaluate the ecological importance of water resources:

  • Habitat Quality: Presence of sensitive or important species
  • Connectivity: Role in larger ecological networks
  • Rarity: Unique or uncommon water feature types
  • Condition: Pristine vs. previously impacted

Water Quality Considerations

Identify water quality factors:

  • Existing Impairments: Already-compromised water bodies
  • Sensitive Parameters: Temperature, sediment, nutrients, etc.
  • Designated Uses: Drinking water, recreation, aquatic life
  • Monitoring Data: Existing water quality information

TRAIL PLANNING STRATEGIES

Watershed-Friendly Trail Alignment

  • Follow Ridgelines: When possible, keep trails on ridges to minimize drainage issues
  • Use Side-Hill Alignments: Contour trails across slopes rather than up/down
  • Avoid Valley Bottoms: Minimize time spent in drainage bottoms
  • Cross Streams Perpendicular: Design crossings at 90Â° to flow direction
  • Respect Buffers: Maintain appropriate distance from water bodies:
    • Streams: Typically 50-100 feet
    • Wetlands: 50-300 feet depending on sensitivity
    • Lakes/Ponds: 100-300 feet

Managing Trail/Water Intersections

Stream Crossing Design Principles

  1. Minimize Number: Reduce total crossings through careful routing
  2. Choose Stable Locations: Select naturally narrow, stable sections with firm banks
  3. Design for Peak Flows: Account for high water events
  4. Provide Clear Approaches: Design approaches to minimize erosion
  5. Consider Aquatic Organisms: Maintain natural stream bed where possible

Crossing Structure Options

Bridges

Minimal in-stream impact, spans entire channel

Culverts

Allows water to pass under trail, requires careful sizing

Armored Fords

Reinforced stream bed for crossing at grade

Stepping Stones

Natural crossing for smaller streams

Boardwalks

Elevated structures for wetland crossings

Drainage Design

Trail Surface Drainage

Outslope

5% cross-slope sheds water from trail

Grade Reversals

Periodic undulations force water off trail

Knicks

Subtle drainage outlets in the trail tread

Rolling Grade Dips

Combination of a knick and a grade reversal

Water Bars

Structures that divert water (use only when necessary)

Managing Upslope Water

  • Swales: Shallow channels that direct water away from trails
  • Berms: Raised areas that prevent water from reaching trails
  • Drains: Structures that capture and redirect water flow
  • Vegetation Management: Maintaining plants that help control water

CONSTRUCTION TECHNIQUES FOR WATERSHED PROTECTION

Erosion and Sediment Control

During Construction

  • Minimize Disturbance Area: Keep clearing and grubbing to minimum
  • Phase Construction: Limit open area at any given time
  • Install Barriers: Silt fences, wattles, or other sediment controls
  • Temporary Stabilization: Mulch or temporary seeding
  • Construction Timing: Work during drier seasons when possible

Post-Construction Stabilization

  • Revegetation: Native species appropriate to site conditions
  • Mulching: Organic materials to protect soil until vegetation establishes
  • Erosion Control Blankets: For steep or sensitive areas
  • Check Dams: Small structures in drainage features to slow water
  • Armoring: Rock placement to protect vulnerable areas

Wetland and Riparian Protection

  • Boardwalk Construction: Techniques to minimize disturbance
  • Helical Piles: Low-impact foundation system
  • Geotextiles: Separation layers to protect sensitive soils
  • Specialized Equipment: Low ground pressure machines
  • Timing Restrictions: Working during frozen or dry periods

MONITORING AND MAINTENANCE

Post-Construction Monitoring

  • Inspection Schedule: Regular checks, especially after storms
  • Photo Points: Established locations for consistent documentation
  • Problem Indicators: Signs of developing issues:
    • Ponding water on trail
    • Sediment accumulation
    • Developing rills or gullies
    • Bank erosion near crossings
    • Structure damage or movement

Adaptive Management

  • Maintenance Prioritization: Focus on water-related issues first
  • Early Intervention: Address small problems before they grow
  • Technique Refinement: Adjust approaches based on performance
  • Seasonal Closures: Consider temporary closures during wet periods
  • Reroutes: Relocate persistently problematic trail sections

Water Quality Monitoring

  • Simple Assessments: Visual clarity, presence of algae, etc.
  • Citizen Science: Volunteer monitoring programs
  • Professional Monitoring: Partnership with agencies or universities
  • Parameters to Consider: Turbidity, temperature, macroinvertebrates

CASE STUDIES

Case Study 1: Mountain Ridge Trail System

Challenge: Trail system in steep terrain with numerous stream crossings

Approach:

  • Comprehensive GIS watershed analysis before design
  • Strategic bridge placements at major crossings
  • Frequent grade reversals on all slopes
  • Designated "wet weather routes" on ridgelines

Outcome: Sustainable trail system with minimal maintenance needs and protected water quality

Case Study 2: Wetland Boardwalk Project

Challenge: Providing access through sensitive wetland complex

Approach:

  • Detailed wetland delineation and functional assessment
  • Elevated boardwalk design with minimal footprint
  • Construction during winter frozen conditions
  • Interpretive elements educating users about wetland values

Outcome: Popular trail feature that provides access while protecting and showcasing sensitive resources

CONCLUSION

Thorough watershed analysis is a critical foundation for sustainable trail planning. By understanding how water moves through the landscape, trail builders can design systems that protect water resources while creating enjoyable, low-maintenance trails. The investment in proper analysis and planning pays dividends through reduced maintenance costs, minimized environmental impacts, and enhanced user experiences.

Remember that watersheds are dynamic systems that change over time. Climate change is altering precipitation patterns in many regions, potentially affecting the validity of historical data. Design with flexibility and resilience in mind, and be prepared to adapt management approaches as conditions change.

This guide is intended as an educational resource for trail planners and builders. For complex projects or those in sensitive watersheds, consider consulting with hydrologists, soil scientists, or other water resource professionals.
