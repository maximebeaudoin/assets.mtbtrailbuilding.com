SOIL ASSESSMENT GUIDE FOR TRAIL PLANNING

INTRODUCTION

Soil conditions are one of the most critical factors in sustainable trail design. This guide provides mountain bike trail builders with practical methods to assess soil characteristics, interpret findings, and make informed decisions about trail routing, construction techniques, and maintenance requirements.

WHY SOIL MATTERS

Soil properties directly influence:

  • Trail Sustainability: How well the trail surface resists erosion and user impacts
  • Construction Methods: What techniques and tools will be most effective
  • Drainage Behavior: How water moves through and across the trail
  • Maintenance Needs: Frequency and type of maintenance required
  • User Experience: Traction, firmness, and overall ride quality
  • Environmental Impact: Potential for erosion, sedimentation, and habitat disruption

Understanding soil characteristics allows you to design trails that work with natural conditions rather than against them.

BASIC SOIL PROPERTIES

Soil Texture

Soil texture refers to the proportion of sand, silt, and clay particles:

Sand (0.05-2.0mm)

Drains quickly, low cohesion, feels gritty

Silt (0.002-0.05mm)

Moderate drainage, moderate cohesion, feels smooth like flour

Clay (<0.002mm)

Drains poorly, high cohesion, feels sticky when wet

The ideal soil for trails typically contains a mix of all three (loam), providing both drainage and stability.

Soil Structure

Structure refers to how soil particles clump together:

  • Granular: Small, rounded aggregates; typically good for trails
  • Blocky: Cube-like aggregates; can be suitable depending on size
  • Platy: Flat, plate-like layers; often problematic for drainage
  • Massive: No visible aggregates; often problematic for trails

Organic Content

Organic matter affects soil behavior:

  • High organic content: Soft, spongy, decomposes over time
  • Low organic content: More stable but may lack cohesion
  • Topsoil: Upper layer rich in organics, typically removed during construction
  • Subsoil: Lower layer with less organic matter, often used for trail tread

FIELD ASSESSMENT TECHNIQUES

Simple Hand Tests

Texture by Feel Method

  1. Take a small handful of soil
  2. Remove rocks, roots, and large organic matter
  3. Add water gradually and knead until moist (like putty)
  4. Follow this decision tree:

    a. Try to form a ribbon by squeezing between thumb and forefinger:

    • Soil falls apart: Sandy soil
    • Forms weak ribbon (<1 inch): Loamy soil
    • Forms strong ribbon (>1 inch): Clay soil

    b. For sandy soil, feel for grittiness:

    • Very gritty: Sand
    • Somewhat gritty: Loamy sand

    c. For loamy soil, feel for grittiness and silkiness:

    • Gritty: Sandy loam
    • Smooth and silky: Silt loam
    • Somewhat sticky: Clay loam

Ball Test

  1. Form a ball of moist soil about 1 inch in diameter
  2. Toss it gently 1 foot in the air and catch
  3. Observe results:
    • Ball breaks apart: Sandy soil (poor cohesion)
    • Ball cracks but holds together: Loamy soil (good cohesion)
    • Ball remains intact: Clay soil (high cohesion)

Drainage Tests

Percolation Test

  1. Dig a hole 6 inches deep and 6 inches wide
  2. Fill with water and let drain completely
  3. Refill and measure time to drain:
    • <10 minutes: Rapid drainage (sandy)
    • 10-30 minutes: Moderate drainage (loamy)
    • > 30 minutes: Slow drainage (clay)

Puddle Observation

After rain, observe how water behaves on potential trail corridors:

  • Quick disappearance: Well-drained soil
  • Standing water: Poorly drained soil
  • Water flowing on surface: Potential erosion issues

Soil Profile Assessment

Test Pit Method

  1. Dig a small pit 12-18 inches deep
  2. Examine soil layers (horizons):
    • Note thickness of topsoil
    • Identify changes in color and texture
    • Look for hardpan layers or bedrock
    • Check for mottling (spots of different colors indicating seasonal water table)

Slope Cut Examination

  1. Find existing cuts in the landscape (road cuts, stream banks)
  2. Observe soil profile characteristics
  3. Note how existing cuts are performing (stable vs. eroding)

INTERPRETING RESULTS FOR TRAIL DESIGN

Soil Texture Implications

Soil Type Strengths Weaknesses Design Considerations
Sandy Good drainage, doesn't get muddy Low cohesion, erodes easily Gentle grades (<7%), frequent grade reversals, may need stabilization
Loamy Good balance of drainage and stability Moderate erosion resistance Moderate grades (7-10%), standard drainage features
Clay High stability when dry Poor drainage, sticky when wet, shrinks/swells Enhanced drainage features, consider seasonal closures, armoring in key areas
Rocky Very durable, natural armoring Difficult construction, poor soil development Can handle steeper grades, requires specialized construction techniques

Drainage Implications

Rapid Low mud potential, high displacement potential Contain loose material with edge barriers, maintain outslope

Very Slow Significant challenges, often unsuitable Consider rerouting, or use boardwalks and extensive drainage

ADVANCED ASSESSMENT METHODS

Laboratory Testing

For large or high-investment projects, consider professional soil testing:

  • Particle size analysis: Precise soil texture determination
  • Organic content measurement: Exact percentage of organic matter
  • Compaction testing: Soil density and compaction potential
  • Erosion potential: Specialized tests for erodibility

GIS and Remote Sensing

  • Soil survey maps: USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS) soil maps
  • LiDAR data: High-resolution topography revealing drainage patterns
  • Aerial imagery: Historical patterns of vegetation and moisture
  • Slope analysis: Identifying optimal trail corridors based on slope

SOIL AMENDMENTS AND TRAIL HARDENING

Based on soil assessment, you may need to modify soil properties:

For Sandy Soils

  • Add clay or crushed stone with fines to improve cohesion
  • Consider soil stabilizers for high-traffic areas
  • Use edge containment to prevent displacement

For Clay Soils

  • Add angular aggregate to improve drainage and reduce stickiness
  • Consider geotextile underlayment in wet areas
  • Implement proper crowning or outsloping for surface drainage

Hardening Techniques

  • Aggregate surfacing: Various crushed stone materials
  • Soil cement: Mixing native soil with portland cement
  • Geotextiles: Stabilization fabrics under trail tread
  • Rock armoring: Natural stone placement in problem areas
  • Boardwalks: Elevated structures for very poor soils

SEASONAL CONSIDERATIONS

Soil behavior changes with moisture content:

  • Spring conditions: Often saturated, maximum plasticity
  • Summer conditions: Dry, may be dusty or hard
  • Fall conditions: Moderate moisture, often ideal for assessment
  • Winter conditions: Freeze/thaw cycles create unique challenges

Plan assessment during multiple seasons if possible, and design for worst-case conditions.

CASE STUDIES

Case Study 1: Sandy Ridge Trail System, Oregon

Soil Challenge: Highly variable volcanic soils with both clay and sandy areas

Assessment Method: Comprehensive soil mapping before design

Solution: Trail routing specifically matched to soil types, with different construction techniques for different soil zones

Outcome: Highly sustainable trail system with minimal maintenance issues

Case Study 2: Duluth Traverse, Minnesota

Soil Challenge: Thin soils over bedrock with poor drainage in many areas

Assessment Method: Test pits and GIS analysis

Solution: Extensive rock armoring, elevated tread, and seasonal trail closures

Outcome: Sustainable trail network in challenging conditions

CONCLUSION

Thorough soil assessment is a foundational step in sustainable trail design. By understanding the properties and behavior of soils in your project area, you can make informed decisions that lead to trails that are more durable, require less maintenance, and have minimal environmental impact. Remember that soil conditions can vary significantly even within small areas, so thorough assessment throughout your project corridor is essential.

This guide is intended as an educational resource for trail builders. For large-scale or environmentally sensitive projects, consider consulting with soil scientists or geotechnical professionals.
